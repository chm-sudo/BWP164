Mathematical Reminder:

The mathematical function for pH is defined as: $\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]$

To isolate the concentration value, inverse the logarithm: $[H^+] = 10^{-\text{pH}}$

💡

Chemistry Hints

When you add distilled water to dilute an acidic solution, consider these mathematical and chemical relationships:

1. Strong Acids (Fully Dissociated)

Because they ionize completely ($\approx 100\%$), a $10\text{x}$ dilution reduces the $[H^+]$ concentration exactly by 10 times. Since $\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]$, this results in a **precise pH increase of $1.0$**.

2. Weak Acids (Partially Dissociated)

They establish an equilibrium: $HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-$. Diluting with water drives the reaction to shift right (Le Chatelier's Principle) to generate *additional* $H^+$ ions. This counteracts the dilution, meaning the pH will **increase by significantly less than $1.0$** (typically around $0.5$ for $10\text{x}$ dilution).

3. Neutral or Buffered Systems

Pure neutral water ($\text{pH} = 7$) or specialized chemical buffers maintain a stable chemical environment resistant to dilution changes.
